Evaluation of the grand-canonical partition function using expanded
Wang-Landau simulations. I. Thermodynamic properties in the bulk
and at the liquid-vapor phase boundary
Caroline Desgranges and Jerome Delhommelle
Department of Chemistry, University of North Dakota, Grand Forks, North Dakota 58202, USA
(Received 16 January 2012; accepted 5 March 2012; published online 11 May 2012)
TheWang-Landau sampling is a powerful method that allows for a direct determination of the density
of states. However, applications to the calculation of the thermodynamic properties of realistic fluids
have been limited so far. By combining the Wang-Landau method with expanded grand-canonical
simulations, we obtain a high-accuracy estimate for the grand-canonical partition function for atomic
and molecular fluids. Then, using the formalism of statistical thermodynamics, we are able to calculate
the thermodynamic properties of these systems, for a wide range of conditions spanning the
single-phase regions as well as the vapor-liquid phase boundary. Excellent agreement with prior
simulation work and with the available experimental data is obtained for argon and CO2, thereby
establishing the accuracy of the method for the calculation of thermodynamic properties such as free
energies and entropies. © 2012 American Institute of Physics. [http://dx.doi.org/10.1063/1.4712023]
I. INTRODUCTION
A few years ago, Wang and Landau proposed a particularly
promising method.1, 2 The purpose of the Wang-
Landau (WL) approach is different from conventional simulation
methods. Instead of calculating equilibrium properties
for a given set of thermodynamic conditions, the WL
method determines directly the density of states of the system.
This approach consists in dynamically updating the density
of states (i.e., the degeneracy of energy levels) every
time the simulation visits a configuration of the system of a
given energy. Then, using the formalism of statistical thermodynamics,
all thermodynamic properties of the system
can be calculated over a wide range of conditions. The WL
method is very appealing since a single simulation run yields
the thermodynamic properties of the system for a wide range
of conditions. Moreover, unlike conventional simulations, the
WL scheme ensures that all energies available to the system
are sampled evenly, alleviating the need for additional enhanced
sampling techniques. Finally, we add that other methods,
such as multicanonical simulations,3, 4 can also give an
estimate for the density of states. However, as pointed out
by Shell et al.,5 the WL method has the advantage of providing
a density of states which can be readily used to calculate
the thermodynamic properties, given the direct connection
of the WL density of states to the entropy of the
system.
In recent years, the WL method was extended to offlattice
simulations,5, 6 generalized to different statistical
ensembles7, 8 and applied to study a variety of phenomena,7, 9
including the determination of the conditions for phase
coexistence.8, 10–16 Despite its conceptual advantages and its
simplicity of use, applications of the WL approach have been
limited, partly because of its perceived lack of precision when
compared to other methods such as the transition matrix (TM)
method.17–21 For instance, Ganzenmüller and Camp10 used
the WL method in the grand-canonical ensemble to study the
vapor-liquid equilibria of the Lennard-Jones fluid. While they
reported densities for the two coexisting phases in good agreement
with the TM results, they also obtained inaccurate results
for the activity and the chemical potential (most notably
at low temperatures for which the deviation from the TM results
reached 10%). As noted by Ganzenmüller and Camp,
the error was intrinsic to the simulation method since it could
not be reduced by varying the convergence parameters used
in the WL calculation. This is a significant drawback since
low-quality estimates for the chemical potential, and hence
for the partition function of the system, precludes the direct
calculation of the thermodynamic properties for the simulated
system.
In this work, we show how we use the WL method to obtain
a high-accuracy estimate for the grand-canonical function
of atomic and molecular fluids. More specifically, we discuss
in Sec. II how we carry out aWL sampling of a simplified version
of the expanded grand-canonical ensemble,22–30 which
consists in dividing the insertion and deletion of entire atoms
and molecules in a large number of intermediate stages. We
discuss the Metropolis criteria obtained for the resulting simulation
method.We then apply this method to determine a large
number of thermodynamic properties, including the entropy,
which is notoriously difficult to determine, for argon and carbon
dioxide in the bulk (liquid or vapor) phases as well as at
the vapor-liquid phase boundary.We establish the accuracy of
the method by comparing our results to prior simulation work,
when available, and to experimental data, most notably for the
entropy of the bulk phases and at coexistence.We finally draw
the main conclusions from this work.

As can be seen from the equations above, the chemical

means that we do not need to specify a given value for μ to
run the simulation. TheWang-Landau sampling scheme leads
to a self-consistent determination of Q(N,V, T, l). At the beginning
of the simulation, we impose Q(N,V, T, l) = 1 for
all N and l. During the simulation, whenever the system visits
a configuration with N full particles and a fractional particle

it by f, a convergence factor greater than 1). Throughout
the simulation, we collect a histogram of the number of
visits for each N and l. Once this histogram is close enough

for the number of visit is initialized and the simulation is run
again with this new convergence factor. We add that the convergence
of the Wang-Landau algorithm has been formally
proved34 and that there is a considerable freedom in choosing
a criterion for estimating whether the histogram H(V )
is flat. This process is repeated until f reaches a minimum
threshold value, leading to an accurate numerical estimate for
Q(N,V, T, l) for all N and l. Since the Wang-Landau algorithm
provides Q(N,V, T, l) up to a multiplicative factor, we

C. Simulation details
We combine the Wang-Landau sampling scheme with
Monte Carlo simulations. For argon, the Monte Carlo (MC)
steps consist in either the translation of a single atom (75%
of the MC steps) or in a change in (N, l) (25% of the MC
steps). For CO2, the MC steps are either the translation of a
single molecule (37.5% of the MC steps), the rotation of a
single molecule (37.5% of the MC steps) or in a change in
(N, l) (25% of the MC steps). The initial value for the convergence
factor is set to e. After every iteration of the process, the
value of the convergence factor is reduced by taking its square
root until the minimal threshold value (f = 10−8) is reached.
As noted by Ganzenüller and Camp,10 the convergence of the
Wang-Landau algorithm has been formally proved34 and there
is a considerable freedom in choosing a criterion for estimating
whether the histogram H(V ) is flat. In this work, we use
the same criterion as in Ref. 10 (i.e., that all values of N and
l have been visited at least 1000 times) to determine that the
Wang-Landau simulation has converged for a given value of
the convergence factor. The simplicity of this method lies in
the fact that we do not need to specify any value for the chemical
potential to run the simulation. Moreover, we only need to
run a single simulation to have access to the partition function
at a given temperature.

partition function of atomic and molecular fluids.

Prior applications to the calculation of the thermodynamic
properties of realistic fluids had been limited, partly because
of a lack of accuracy of the partition function so obtained.
By combining the WL sampling with a simplified version of
the expanded grand-canonical ensemble, we show how the
grand-canonical partition function of the system can be evaluated.
Then, using the formalism of statistical thermodynamics,
thermodynamic properties such as the entropy and the
Helmholtz and Gibbs free energies are determined for argon
and carbon dioxide over a wide range of conditions. We establish
the accuracy of the method by comparing our results
to prior simulation work, when available, and to experimental
data in the bulk phases and at coexistence. We emphasize
that the method presented in this work is general and can be
applied to any kind of molecular fluid. This method is also
simple to use since a single simulation run yields the thermodynamic
properties of the system at a given temperature.
